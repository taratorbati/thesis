Here is a complete narrative of the architectural journey we went through to arrive at the final MPC design. This summarizes the back-and-forth debates, the computational bottlenecks we hit, and the specific reasons we pivoted to the final JAX-based framework. 

***

### 1. The Initial Concept: Centralized Sparse NLP
We began the control design aiming for the traditional gold standard in optimal control: formulating the problem for a centralized, sparse Non-Linear Programming (NLP) solver. The initial goal was to map out the entire state-space (the five variables $x_1$ through $x_5$) across all 130 agents and feed it into an off-the-shelf solver to compute the exact optimal irrigation sequence over the prediction horizon $H_p$.

**The Bottleneck:** We quickly realized that while this works beautifully for a single crop model, scaling it to a 130-agent topological grid caused a computational explosion. The elevation-based routing (the Directed Acyclic Graph) meant the agents were deeply coupled. Calculating the massive Jacobian matrices required to solve this non-convex problem across the full finite-horizon space $\mathbb{R}^{N \cdot H_p}$ became computationally intractable. It was too heavy, too slow, and completely unviable for deployment on real-world edge devices at the farm level.

### 2. The Strategic Pivot: JAX and Differentiable Simulation
To break the computational bottleneck, we had to rethink the core engine. We debated whether to simplify the physics (which we ultimately rejected, as proven by your recent runoff validation) or change the optimization paradigm. We chose the latter.

Instead of passing the equations to an external NLP solver, we decided to rewrite the entire environment as a **differentiable simulation using JAX**. This was a major architectural shift. By natively compiling the crop-soil dynamics and the DAG routing into JAX, we could leverage automatic differentiation to calculate the gradients of the entire 130-agent farm simultaneously. This massively accelerated the forward prediction and backward gradient passes.

**The Theoretical Trade-off:** We explicitly discussed that making this shift meant sacrificing the formal recursive stability guarantees that come with classical NLP frameworks. However, we justified this trade-off because agricultural systems operate on slow, daily time steps. The microsecond-level stability required for aerospace control is unnecessary here; what we needed was computational tractability, which the JAX architecture delivered.

### 3. Solving the Constraint Problem: Projected Gradient Descent
Moving away from an NLP solver introduced a new problem: we lost the built-in ability to handle strict physical constraints. The AI optimizer, simply following the gradient to maximize yield, might try to apply negative irrigation or use an infinite amount of water. 

To solve this, we architected a multi-layered constraint handling system:
* **Action Feasibility:** We implemented **Projected Gradient Descent**. After every optimization step, the algorithm physically clamps the control inputs to respect the actuator box limits (e.g., $u \ge 0$ and $u \le \text{max\_pipe\_capacity}$).
* **Dense Global Water Budget:** We added a projection step to ensure that the sum of all irrigation applied across the 130 agents in a given day does not exceed the farm's total available water supply.
* **Surrogate State Feasibility:** To handle internal state boundaries—like preventing the soil moisture ($x_1$) from exceeding saturation or penalizing surface flooding ($x_5$)—we introduced **interior-point barriers**. Instead of treating these as hard mathematical walls that would break the gradient descent, we transformed them into steep penalties within the surrogate state, guiding the optimizer smoothly away from unphysical or agronomically harmful decisions.

### 4. Refining the Objective Cost Function ($J$)
Throughout this process, we continuously refined what the controller should actually care about. The initial idea of simply "maximizing yield and minimizing water" was too naive for physical deployment. 

We arrived at a non-convex optimal control formulation that balances three competing forces:
1.  **Terminal Crop Biomass:** Maximizing the final $x_4$ state at the end of the season to ensure agricultural profitability.
2.  **Irrigation Water Costs:** Minimizing the sum of $u$ to respect the dense global water budget and conserve resources.
3.  **Control Variations ($\Delta u$):** We introduced a penalty for "chattering." We realized that if the AI constantly swings the physical irrigation valves from 0% to 100% every single day, it would cause severe mechanical wear and tear. Penalizing $\Delta u$ forces the optimizer to prefer smooth, stable irrigation schedules.

### 5. The Final Synergy: Why the Sandbox Matters
This entire architectural debate is exactly why we just spent so much effort perfecting the ABM physics. Because the JAX-based MPC uses gradient descent to navigate the environment, the physics engine itself acts as the AI's training ground. If we hadn't mathematically decoupled the surface layer to model localized valley flooding ($x_5$), the interior-point barriers would have had nothing to penalize, and the differentiable simulator would have learned to continuously drown the sink agents.
